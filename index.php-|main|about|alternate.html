Finnegans Wake is a text marked by music, yet this simple fact has long been obscured by the forbidding layering of Joyce’s prose. A text that should be appreciated for its lyrical assonance and rhythms is regarded as a notorious symbol for impenetrability and displeasure. Anyone who has heard Joyce read the novel’s ending can appreciate the book’s integral aural component; anyone appreciating Joyce’s penchant for bawdy limericks, and glee in the coarse double-entendre of naming a book of poetry (Chamber Music) after the “music” of a chamber pot, can understand his desire for unconventional music.

Roland McHugh’s Annotations to Finnegans Wake aligns this musicality with Joyce’s experimentation in referentiality and linguistic layering by arranging his annotations spatially, such that they resemble sheet music. Jail of Mounty takes this logic a step further by literally playing the annotations as music. I accomplish this by creating larger categories of references—-e.g. "Professional Jargon," "Linguistic [Taxonomic],” "Geographic”—-and aligning each category to a respective octave. Readers can then perceive, aurally, when certain references are being invoked, and their density. As McHugh breaks a single line of the Wake into two lines of annotations, I have translated each line from the Wake into a second-long interval, in order for the resulting sound to be legible.

Further, the questions of density, and its easy perceivability, is one at heard of understanding the book’s construction. As is by now canonical, Joyce how Ulysses was a book of the day, and Finnegans Wake a book of the night, with all unclarities implied by this nocturnal state (Ellmann, James Joyce, 1983, pg 590). Critics such as John Bishop have cemented the reality of the Wake as mimetically presenting the course of a night, and the book’s correlated frenzy of linguistic density, amalgamations, and increased impenetrability as representing the brain’s passage from REM to deep sleep. Jail of Mountjoy sampled from the books of the Wake accordingly to illustrate this passage of dream states sonically; one can make in a clearer fashion the density, and related brain state, of Joyce’s “night music” (as Anthony Burgess reflects in Re-Joyce).

Chance combinations—so long at the heart of artists engaging with Joyce’s work—will also occur, yielding new and unforeseen harmonies and melodies. In this case I take the initiative from John Cage’s intentionally chance-based "Roaratorio" and "The Merry Widow of Eighteen Springs.” Also, users will find certain almost subliminal, and certainly sub-textual, figurative dimensions to Joyce’s use of referential density. When the light begins to emerge in the Wake’s final book, where the mythological female figure ALP is returning from river to ocean, the aching lucidity and thinness of sound reiterate thematic elements of the work: the exhausted tones of a lover, the great stillness of a Biblical-level flood and oceanic expanse.
